The missing Cmd+X
for macOS Finder.

macOS has never had a proper cut-and-move in Finder. Cut & Move fixes that — a tiny menu-bar utility that turns Cmd+X and Cmd+V into a real cut-and-paste, the way you've always expected it to work.

How it works

Three keystrokes. That's it.

Cut & Move only activates when Finder is frontmost. Everywhere else, Cmd+X behaves exactly as always.

  1. Select

    Click the files or folders you want to move in Finder.

    Click

  2. Cut

    Press Cmd+X. The menu bar scissors icon fills in to confirm cut mode is active.

    ⌘X

  3. Move

    Navigate to the destination and press Cmd+V. Files are moved, not copied.

    ⌘V
FAQ

Questions, answered.

Why doesn't macOS Finder have Cmd+X to cut files?

Apple has never shipped a Cmd+X shortcut in Finder. The closest native equivalent is Cmd+C to copy, then Cmd+Option+V to Move Item Here. Cut & Move makes the expected Cmd+X / Cmd+V flow work by converting those keystrokes into Finder's native move operation.

How does Cut & Move work?

Cut & Move installs a low-level CGEvent keyboard tap. When you press Cmd+X in Finder it silently performs a Cmd+C and enters cut mode. When you press Cmd+V, it injects the Option modifier so Finder receives Cmd+Option+V — the native Move Item Here command — relocating the files instead of duplicating them.

Is Cut & Move safe? Why does it need Accessibility permissions?

macOS requires Accessibility permissions for any app that observes keyboard input. Cut & Move only processes events when Finder is the frontmost application, and never records, logs, or transmits keystrokes. The source code is auditable and has zero third-party dependencies.

Does Cut & Move affect other applications?

No. Cut & Move checks that Finder is the frontmost app on every keystroke. In every other application — terminals, browsers, editors, productivity tools — Cmd+X and Cmd+V behave exactly as they always have.

What macOS version does Cut & Move require?

Cut & Move requires macOS 26.1 or later.
